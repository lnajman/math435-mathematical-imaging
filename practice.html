Ungraded Weekly Practice

This page gives optional weekly practice prompts connected to the book, slides, and notebooks.

Important

These activities are for practice and preparation. They are not separate graded assignments unless the instructor explicitly announces otherwise. Course grading is governed by the syllabus.

The purpose is to help students keep a running record of the central habit of the course:

What does the data support, what does the model assume, and where can the reconstruction fail?

Students may keep these notes privately, use them while studying, or bring questions from them to class.

How To Use This Page

For each week:

  • read the connected book chapter;
  • attend class and use the slide deck as the discussion structure;
  • run the notebook in Python or Google Colab;
  • write a short claim-evidence-limit note for yourself.

The notes are useful preparation for quizzes, exams, project work, and the oral defense, but they are not an additional grading category.

Claim-Evidence-Limit Template

Use this short format when a notebook or class discussion produces a reconstruction result.

Claim:

Evidence:

Model assumption:

Limit or failure case:

Example:

Item Example
Claim TV denoising keeps the main edge sharper than quadratic smoothing.
Evidence Gradient maps and edge contrast stay high across three TV weights.
Model assumption The image is approximately piecewise smooth.
Limit or failure case TV creates staircasing on smooth intensity ramps.

Weekly Practice Guide

Week Topic Ungraded Practice Notebook Focus Useful Links
1 Images as mathematical objects Identify x, y, the observation process, and one piece of missing information in a masked image. From Scene to Data book, slides, notebook, Colab
2 Convolution and blur Choose a blur kernel and boundary condition; write one assumption that makes the model reasonable. Blur as a Forward Model book, slides, notebook, Colab
3 Fourier transform in imaging Explain how Fourier coefficients act as global projections and why blur weakens some frequencies. Fourier Projection and Stability book, slides, notebook, Colab
4 Noise models and likelihoods Match Gaussian or Poisson noise to a data-fidelity term and say what averaging can and cannot fix. Likelihood as Data Evidence book, slides, notebook, Colab
5 Ill-posed inverse problems Name one well-measured direction, one unstable direction, and one invisible change. Directions and Evidence book, slides, notebook, Colab
6 Tikhonov regularization Sweep \lambda and explain which observable diagnostic you would report if the true image were unknown. Choosing Lambda Without the Truth book, slides, notebook, Colab
7 Variational formulation Identify the data term, regularizer, and optimality condition for one energy. Energy as an Audit Trail book, slides, notebook, Colab
8 Total Variation regularization Compare one preserved edge with one possible artifact or staircasing failure. Edge Preservation Is Not Proof book, slides, notebook, Colab
9 Optimization methods Report whether energy, fixed-point change, or active coefficients show stable iteration. Optimization Diagnostics book, slides, notebook, Colab
10 Sparse reconstruction State the representation where the image is assumed sparse and design one mismatch example. Representation Mismatch book, slides, notebook, Colab
11 Wavelets and multiscale representation Explain which local features wavelets capture better than global Fourier modes. Fourier, Wavelets, and Energy book, slides, notebook, Colab
12 Model-based vs data-driven imaging Describe what the training data teach the learned prior to expect and test one distribution shift. Training Data as a Prior book, slides, notebook, Colab
13 Plug-and-play and learned regularization Compare data residual, RMSE when available, and fixed-point change for two denoisers. Implicit Prior Strength book, slides, notebook, Colab
14 Stability, robustness, and ethics Write three project-style claims and pair each with evidence and a limitation. From Notebook to Project Defense book, slides, notebook, Colab
15 Project presentations and oral defenses Review the model, code, evidence, limitations, and LLM-use statement for the final project. Not applicable project page, syllabus

Project Preparation Guide

The syllabus defines the assessed project moments as the proposal in Week 6, the report in Week 14, and the oral defense in Week 15. The project notes below are study and planning aids for the final modeling project described on the project page; they do not create additional graded items.

Week Planning Aid
6 Prepare the project proposal: topic, team, data source, baseline, second method, degradation model, and first definition of x, y, and the forward process.
7 Refine the model statement, data access, and implementation choices after feedback.
8 Make sure the notebook can generate or load data and run a simple baseline.
10 Check that both methods run on the same test cases.
12 Add one parameter sweep, one metric, and one robustness or failure test.
14 Prepare the final notebook, written report, claim-evidence table, LLM-use statement, and oral-defense explanations.
