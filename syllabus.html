MATH 435: Mathematical Imaging

(3 Lecture hours, 0 Laboratory, 3 Credits)

Prerequisites

  • MATH 317 – Advanced Calculus

  • MATH 412 – Probability and Statistics

Corequisites

  • None

Course Catalog Description

This course develops the mathematical foundations of imaging science from a modern inverse problems perspective. Students learn how images are formed, degraded, and reconstructed using tools from linear algebra, Fourier analysis, probability, variational modeling, and optimization.

The course emphasizes mathematical modeling, stability analysis, regularization techniques (Tikhonov, Total Variation, sparse methods), and computational implementation. Classical model-based approaches are connected to modern computational imaging techniques, including data-driven and learning-based reconstruction methods.

Applications focus on medical and scientific imaging.

Textbook

  • M Bertero. and P. Boccacci, Introduction to Inverse Problems in Imaging, 2nd ed., CRC Press, 2021.

Reference Materials

  • A. Chambolle and T. Pock, “An introduction to continuous optimization for imaging,” Acta Numerica, vol. 25, pp. 161–319, 2016.

  • S. Mallat, A Wavelet Tour of Signal Processing, 3rd ed., Academic Press, 2008.

  • Selected recent research articles on modern computational imaging and learning-based reconstruction (provided during the course).

Course Structure & Learning Methodology

Lectures will be 3x50 minutes or 2 × 75-minutes per week. The course is delivered through lectures, guided mathematical derivations, conceptual discussions, and computational demonstrations using MATLAB or Python. Homework assignments and an individual modeling project are conducted throughout the semester to reinforce understanding.

The course emphasizes reasoning and modeling rather than formal proof-writing.

Instructor Policy on Late Submission of Assignments

Quizzes

There will be two quizzes: Quiz 1 in Week 4 and Quiz 2 in Week 12. Each quiz is 20 minutes long. A student-facing preparation summary is available on the quiz information page.

There will be no make-up quizzes. If the student fails to attend a scheduled quiz without an approved absence from the Student Success Department, then a grade of zero for that quiz will be assigned.

If the student fails to attend a quiz and the Student Success Department confirms that the student’s absence from that quiz is approved, then the grade that will be assigned to that quiz is:

  • the grade of the Semester examination if that quiz took place before the Semester examination;
  • the grade of the Final Examination if that quiz took place after the Semester examination.

Semester Examination

There will be no make-up Semester Examination. If the student fails to attend the scheduled Semester examination without an approved absence from the Student Success Department, then a grade of zero will be assigned for the Semester examination.

If the student fails to attend the Semester examination and the Student Success Department confirms that the absence from the Semester examination is approved, then the grade for the Final Examination will also be assigned as the grade for the Semester examination.

A maximum of 40% of the course grade can be compensated through the above policy.

Additional Information

Assessment

Late submission of the group project will not be accepted.

Project

The project will be assigned in Week 6 of the semester. Students will work in pairs (teams of two) to formulate, analyze, and solve a mathematical imaging reconstruction problem. Each team will develop an appropriate mathematical model, implement computational reconstruction methods, analyze the results, and evaluate model performance and parameter sensitivity.

Students are required to use Python for all computational implementations and analyses. Students are also required to use an LLM as part of the project process and disclose how it was used. The LLM-use statement is required but not graded separately.

A written report and the corresponding code must be submitted in Week 14.

In Week 15, each team will participate in an individual oral defense. Each student will be examined separately on the modeling decisions, implementation, code, parameter choices, failure analysis, and interpretation of results. Both students are expected to demonstrate full understanding of the entire project. The oral defense and code walkthrough represent 25% of the project grade.

Failure to participate in the oral defense will result in a zero for the oral defense and code walkthrough component.

Detailed Course Plan (15 Weeks, Tentative)

Week 1 – Introduction to Imaging as a Mathematical Problem

Topics

  • What is an image mathematically?

  • Continuous vs discrete images

  • Pixels, grids, vector representation

  • Linear systems viewpoint of imaging

  • Forward operator concept

Focus
Students understand that imaging is a mathematical mapping:

Ax = b

Learning Goal
Students can describe image formation as a mathematical model.

Week 2 – Convolution and Blur

Topics

  • Convolution in 1D and 2D

  • Point spread function (PSF)

  • Physical meaning of blur

  • Boundary conditions

In class

  • Compute simple convolution examples

  • Visual interpretation of blur

Learning Goal
Students can formulate blur as convolution.

Week 3 – Fourier Transform in Imaging

Topics

  • 2D Fourier transform

  • Convolution theorem

  • Frequency interpretation of blur

  • Low-pass vs high-pass filtering

Focus
Why inversion in frequency domain amplifies noise.

Learning Goal
Students understand why deblurring is unstable.

Week 4 – Discrete Fourier Transform & Noise Models

Topics

  • DFT and FFT

  • Spectral representation of images

  • Gaussian noise model

  • Poisson noise (medical relevance)

  • Likelihood formulation

Learning Goal
Students can distinguish noise models and write likelihood expressions.

Week 5 – Ill-Posed Inverse Problems

Topics

  • Hadamard definition of well-posedness

  • Instability of inverse filtering

  • Singular values (conceptual SVD)

  • Condition number

Demonstration
Noise amplification in inverse filtering.

Learning Goal
Students understand why reconstruction requires regularization.

Week 6 – Tikhonov Regularization

Topics

  • ℓ2 regularization

  • Closed-form solution

(A^{T}A + \lambda I)x = A^{T}b

  • Bias–variance tradeoff

  • Parameter influence

Project Assigned

Teams receive dataset + problem description.

Learning Goal
Students understand stabilization through regularization.

Week 7 – Variational Formulation

Topics

  • Energy minimization framework

  • Convexity (geometric interpretation)

  • Euler–Lagrange derivation (guided)

  • Gradient descent intuition

Learning Goal
Students can derive optimality condition for ℓ2 model.

Week 8 – Total Variation (TV) Regularization

Topics

  • Definition of TV norm

  • Edge preservation mechanism

  • Comparison with ℓ2 smoothing

  • Staircasing effect

Midterm Exam

Learning Goal
Students can explain why TV preserves edges better than ℓ2.

Week 9 – Optimization Methods

Topics

  • Convex sets and convex functions

  • Subgradient concept (intuitive)

  • Proximal operator idea

  • Iterative shrinkage algorithm (ISTA intuition)

Learning Goal
Students understand algorithmic structure behind regularization.

Week 10 – Sparse Reconstruction

Topics

  • ℓ1 regularization

  • Sparsity concept

  • Compressed sensing intuition

  • Comparison ℓ1 vs ℓ2

Focus
Why sparsity improves reconstruction in certain bases.

Learning Goal
Students can justify when ℓ1 is preferable.

Week 11 – Wavelets and Multiscale Representation

Topics

  • Wavelet transform concept

  • Sparsity in wavelet domain

  • Thresholding methods

  • Denoising comparison experiments

Learning Goal
Students can connect representation choice to reconstruction quality.

Week 12 – Model-Based vs Data-Driven Imaging

Topics

  • Classical variational reconstruction

  • Learned priors concept

  • Neural networks as parameterized operators

  • Comparison of philosophies

Learning Goal
Students understand the difference between handcrafted and learned regularizers.

Week 13 – Plug-and-Play and Learned Regularization

Topics

  • Iterative algorithms structure

  • Replacing proximal operator with denoiser

  • Plug-and-play priors

  • CNN as denoiser in optimization loop

  • Convergence questions (conceptual)

Learning Goal
Students understand how AI integrates into mathematical frameworks.

Week 14 – Stability, Robustness, and Ethics

Topics

  • Parameter sensitivity analysis

  • Hallucination artifacts in AI reconstruction

  • Overfitting in inverse problems

  • Reliability in medical imaging

  • Societal and ethical implications

Project Reports Due

Learning Goal
Students recognize limitations and ethical considerations.

Week 15 – Oral Defenses

Activities

  • Individual oral defense per student

  • Evaluation of:

    • Modeling understanding

    • Parameter reasoning

    • Interpretation of results

    • Team collaboration

Learning Goal
Students demonstrate independent mastery of project content.
