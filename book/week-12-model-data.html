Model-Based vs Data-Driven Imaging

Chapter 12

Slides | Notebook | Open in Colab

Learning Goals

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • compare model-based and data-driven imaging;
  • identify forward models, priors, solvers, and training data;
  • interpret a neural network as a parameterized operator;
  • explain a small learned patch prior;
  • discuss generalization and failure modes.

Where Does Prior Knowledge Come From?

Wavelets gave us a handcrafted representation. We chose the wavelet family, decomposition level, thresholding rule, and threshold strength.

Data-driven imaging asks a different question: what if some of these choices are learned from examples?

Model-based methods write assumptions; data-driven methods learn assumptions from examples.

Why This Chapter Matters

This chapter is the explicit turning point of the course. Up to now, we have built reconstruction methods by writing down assumptions:

y = Ax+\eta, \qquad \hat{x} = \operatorname*{argmin}_x D(Ax,y)+\lambda R(x).

The data term came from a measurement model. The regularizer came from a mathematical prior: small energy, smoothness, sparse gradients, sparse transform coefficients, or wavelet structure.

Data-driven imaging changes where some of that knowledge comes from. Instead of choosing every part by hand, we use examples to learn a map, a prior, a denoiser, a feature representation, or an algorithmic update.

The central identity of the course appears here:

\text{neural imaging} = \text{inverse problems} + \text{learned image models}.

Neural networks are not outside inverse problems. They are one answer to the same difficulty: the measured data are incomplete, noisy, unstable, or ambiguous, so reconstruction requires additional structure.

This identity is more than a slogan. It says that a neural method should be read with the same questions we used for Tikhonov, TV, sparse reconstruction, and wavelets:

  • What is measured?
  • What is unknown?
  • Which information is missing or unstable?
  • What prior information is being added?
  • Where does that prior information come from?
  • How can we test whether the result is supported by the data?

In a classical method, the prior may be a formula such as \|Lx\|^2, \|\nabla x\|_1, or \|\alpha\|_1 in a wavelet basis. In a learned method, the prior may be spread across the training set, architecture, loss, optimizer, and learned parameters. The mathematical discipline is the same: do not confuse the prior with the measurement.

What Changes, What Does Not

Moving from handcrafted priors to learned priors changes where image knowledge comes from. It does not remove the inverse problem.

Part of reconstruction Classical version Learned version Still required?
measurement model A or f written explicitly sometimes explicit, sometimes implicit yes, if data fidelity matters
data consistency residual or likelihood term loss term, layer, projection, or audit yes
image prior formula such as TV or sparsity training data, architecture, denoiser, learned features yes
computation optimizer or proximal algorithm network inference or learned iteration yes
evidence residuals, sweeps, baselines validation, shift tests, residuals, baselines yes

The learned method may be more expressive, but it is not less responsible. If anything, the responsibility increases because some assumptions become harder to read from a formula.

Model-Based Imaging

A classical reconstruction often solves

\hat{x} = \operatorname*{argmin}_x D(Ax,y)+\lambda R(x).

Here D is data fidelity and R is the regularizer or prior.

In a model-based method, we specify:

  • the forward model A;
  • the noise model or data term;
  • the regularizer;
  • the parameter \lambda;
  • the optimization method.

Examples from this course include Tikhonov regularization, TV, l1 sparse reconstruction, and wavelet thresholding.

Model-based methods are often interpretable, grounded in physics, usable with limited training data, and easier to analyze mathematically. They can struggle when the forward model is incomplete or the handcrafted prior is too simple.

The advantage of explicit modeling is that each assumption can be inspected. If a reconstruction fails, we can ask whether the forward model was wrong, the noise model was wrong, the regularizer was too simple, or the parameter was badly chosen.

The limitation is also clear. Handcrafted priors are usually simple because they must be written in a short mathematical form and optimized. Natural images contain structure beyond smoothness, TV, or wavelet sparsity: repeated patterns, semantic objects, textures, anatomy, material boundaries, and long-range dependencies. Model-based methods may not capture these structures well.

What Model-Based Does Not Mean

Model-based does not mean old-fashioned or inferior. It means the reconstruction rule contains explicit assumptions about the measurement and image. Many excellent modern methods remain model-based.

Model-based also does not require the whole forward process to be linear. We often write Ax because it is mathematically clear, but a forward model can be nonlinear:

y=f(x)+\eta.

The important idea is that the reconstruction respects how the data were produced.

Data-Driven Imaging

Instead of writing the reconstruction rule by hand, a data-driven method learns a function:

\hat{x}=f_\theta(y).

The parameters \theta are chosen from training data.

With supervised training pairs (y_i,x_i), one solves

\min_\theta \sum_i \ell(f_\theta(y_i),x_i).

The loss tells the network what counts as a good reconstruction on the training set.

Data-driven methods still require choices:

  • training data;
  • architecture;
  • loss function;
  • optimizer;
  • evaluation metric;
  • deployment domain.

The prior is no longer a short expression such as TV. It is represented by parameters learned from examples.

This means that a learned method is not assumption-free. It has simply moved many assumptions from the page into the training process. A convolutional network assumes that local filters and repeated spatial patterns are useful. A U-Net assumes that multiscale features and skip connections are useful. A training set of face images assumes that test images will share face-like structures. A loss such as mean squared error assumes that pixelwise average error is the right quantity to minimize.

Those assumptions can be excellent. They can also be wrong. The point is not to avoid assumptions; reconstruction is impossible without them. The point is to name them.

The supervised learning problem should be read as learning an inverse map from examples. The training data contain pairs:

\text{degraded or measured data } y_i \quad\longleftrightarrow\quad \text{desired image } x_i.

The network is trained so that f_\theta(y_i) is close to x_i under the chosen loss. After training, the same map is applied to new measurements.

This is powerful, but it changes the meaning of the reconstruction. The output is not only determined by the new measurement y. It is also shaped by the training set, the architecture, and the loss.

Learned Inverse Maps

The simplest data-driven imaging picture is

\hat{x}=f_\theta(y).

This is a learned inverse map. It tries to approximate the transformation from data to clean image directly.

For denoising, y may be a noisy image and x a clean image. For super-resolution, y may be a low-resolution image and x a high-resolution image. For reconstruction, y may be sensor measurements and x the desired image.

The risk is that a direct learned inverse may ignore the physics of the measurement. If the network produces an image that looks plausible but does not satisfy Ax\approx y, then the image is not faithful to the measured data.

This is the main conceptual difference between image synthesis and image reconstruction. A synthesis method may be judged by whether the output looks plausible. A reconstruction method must also be judged by whether the output is compatible with the observation.

For a learned inverse map, the forward model gives a simple audit:

\hat{x}=f_\theta(y), \qquad \text{then check} \qquad A\hat{x}\approx y.

If this check fails beyond the expected noise and modeling error, the network may have produced a good-looking image that is not a good reconstruction. If the check passes, the reconstruction is still not automatically true, because many images may satisfy the same measurement. But data consistency is a necessary guardrail.

What Can Be Learned?

Different neural imaging methods learn different objects. It is useful to separate them.

Learned object Mathematical role Example question
inverse map f_\theta(y) direct reconstruction rule Can the network map noisy data to a clean image?
prior or regularizer R_\theta(x) learned image preference Which images should be considered plausible?
denoiser D_\theta(z) implicit proximal-like step Can a learned denoiser replace a handcrafted prior step?
feature representation \Phi_\theta(x) learned coordinates Which features make comparison meaningful?
algorithmic update G_\theta learned iteration Can the network learn how to improve an iterate?

These are not the same mathematical object. A direct inverse map may be fast, but it can hide data inconsistency. A learned prior or denoiser can be inserted into an inverse-problem algorithm. An unrolled update can use both the current image and the data residual. Each design choice answers the inverse problem in a different way.

For this course, the safest mental model is:

\text{measured data} + \text{learned prior information} \rightarrow \text{reconstruction to be tested}.

The learned part is powerful because it can encode richer image structure than a small handcrafted formula. It is risky because the learned structure may not belong to the image being reconstructed.

Strengths And Risks

Data-driven methods can exploit complex image statistics, be fast at inference time, adapt to a specific scanner or modality, and outperform handcrafted priors when training and test data match.

They can fail when:

  • the test distribution changes;
  • training data is biased;
  • the loss rewards the wrong thing;
  • uncertainty is hidden;
  • hallucinated structure looks plausible.

The last point matters especially in medical and scientific imaging: plausible is not the same as measured.

The phrase “hallucination” means that the method adds structure that was not supported by the measurement. In a low-stakes photographic application, this may be acceptable or even desired. In scientific imaging, medical imaging, forensics, or measurement-driven tasks, it can be dangerous.

The problem is not that learned methods are uniquely untrustworthy. Handcrafted priors can also hallucinate in a quieter way: TV can create artificial edges or plateaus, wavelets can remove texture, and Tikhonov can oversmooth. The difference is that learned priors can encode complex training-set expectations that are harder to see directly.

This is why a learned reconstruction should be described with two verbs:

  • it fits the measured data;
  • it resembles the training examples.

Good performance often requires both. If the method fits the data but has no useful prior, the reconstruction may be noisy or unstable. If it resembles the training examples but ignores the data, the output may be plausible but unsupported. The tension between these two verbs is the neural version of the classical data-prior tradeoff.

Neural Networks As Operators

A neural network is a parameterized map from input data to output image.

At a high level, layers combine linear filtering or mixing, nonlinear activations, many learned parameters, and composition of simple operations.

A composition of only linear layers is still linear. Nonlinear activations allow the map to adapt to image content.

Some methods ignore the explicit forward model:

\hat{x}=f_\theta(y).

Others include it inside an iterative reconstruction:

x^{k+1} = \text{learned step using } A,\ A^\top,\ y.

This second viewpoint leads naturally to plug-and-play and learned regularization.

Hybrid Methods

Many successful imaging methods are neither purely model-based nor purely data-driven. They combine:

  • explicit forward model A;
  • explicit data-consistency step;
  • learned denoiser, prior, or update rule;
  • iterative algorithm structure;
  • validation on representative data.

A typical hybrid idea is:

x^{k+1} = \text{learned correction} \left(x^k,\ A^\top(Ax^k-y)\right).

The term A^\top(Ax^k-y) tells the network how the current reconstruction disagrees with the data. The learned part decides how to correct the image using training-set knowledge.

This is the safest conceptual bridge to neural imaging: keep the physics where it is known, and learn the image model where handcrafted priors are too limited.

A Small Learned Patch Prior

Learning does not require a large neural network. A simple patch PCA prior already illustrates the main idea.

We extract many clean image patches and learn:

  • a mean patch;
  • principal patch directions;
  • a low-dimensional patch model.

Patch PCA learns common directions of variation from examples.

For each noisy patch, denoising can:

  1. subtract the learned mean patch;
  2. project onto the first few learned directions;
  3. reconstruct the patch;
  4. average overlapping reconstructed patches.

This is data-driven even though it is not a neural network.

The patch PCA example is important because it removes the mystery from “learning.” We are not yet using a deep network. We are learning a low-dimensional representation from clean patches. The learned components are the directions of variation that appear often in the training patches.

This lets students see the basic ingredients of data-driven imaging without the complexity of a large neural architecture:

  • collect examples;
  • choose a model class;
  • fit parameters;
  • apply the fitted model to new data;
  • test whether it generalizes.

The same logic appears in deep learning, only with a much larger model class. A deep network may learn millions of parameters instead of a few principal directions, but the questions remain familiar. What examples were used? What variation did they contain? What loss was minimized? What happens when the test image is different?

The patch PCA example is therefore a bridge. It shows that learning is not magic. It is fitting a model from examples, then using that model as prior information in reconstruction.

Handcrafted and learned priors can be compared on the same denoising task.

Capacity And Generalization

The number of retained PCA components controls model capacity.

The error curve has a useful middle region: too little capacity removes image detail, while too much capacity begins to preserve noise.

If the learned prior is trained on one image family and tested on another, performance can change.

Generalization is central to learned imaging.

Distribution shift means the test data differ from the training data. A learned prior is not universal just because it is learned.

Distribution shift can be subtle. A denoiser trained on natural photographs may fail on microscopy. A network trained on one scanner may fail on another scanner. A super-resolution method trained on one degradation model may fail when the real blur or noise is different.

This is why training data are part of the method. In a paper or project, one should describe not only the network but also what it was trained on and how the test data differ from the training data.

Training Data As A Prior

Training data act like a prior, even when no regularization term is written down.

A learned model is shaped by:

Training choice Prior effect
image family which structures are expected
degradation model which inverse problem is learned
loss function which errors are penalized
augmentation which variations are treated as normal
architecture which computations are easy for the model

This does not make learned methods bad. It makes them honest mathematical objects: they encode assumptions, only in a different form from TV, wavelets, or Tikhonov.

When evaluating a learned reconstruction, ask what the training process made likely. Then ask whether the test case belongs to that world.

Comparing The Philosophies

Question Model-based Data-driven
Prior comes from formulas and modeling examples and loss
Interpretability usually higher often lower
Data requirement lower higher
Physics explicit sometimes implicit
Failure mode wrong model or prior distribution shift or hallucination

Many modern methods combine both philosophies: explicit forward model, learned denoiser or prior, iterative algorithm structure, and data-consistency steps.

Data Consistency

Data consistency means that the reconstruction should agree with the measurements under the forward model:

A\hat{x}\approx y.

This check is not the whole evaluation, because noisy data should not be fit perfectly. But it is essential. If two reconstructions look equally plausible, the one that better respects the measured data is usually more trustworthy.

For a learned method, data consistency can be:

  • enforced by architecture;
  • included as a loss term;
  • applied as a projection or correction step;
  • monitored as an evaluation metric;
  • used inside an unrolled algorithm.

This is one reason inverse-problem thinking remains useful in deep learning. The forward model gives us a way to test the output against the actual measurement.

A Neural Network Is Still An Inverse-Problem Choice

It is tempting to draw a sharp line:

\text{classical method} \quad\text{versus}\quad \text{neural network}.

For mathematical imaging, a better line is:

\text{explicit prior} \quad\text{versus}\quad \text{learned prior}.

Both live inside the inverse problem. Both can be useful. Both can fail. The neural network changes how the prior is represented and how it is fitted, but it does not remove the need for a forward model, a noise model, validation, and careful interpretation.

This is the main reason the course naturally leads to neural networks. Once students understand that reconstruction is impossible from data alone, the next question is unavoidable:

If handcrafted priors are too simple, can the prior be learned?

That question is the door from classical inverse problems to modern neural imaging.

Evaluation

Pixel RMSE is not enough. We may also need:

  • task accuracy;
  • uncertainty estimates;
  • robustness to distribution shift;
  • consistency with measured data;
  • expert review.

This is the doorway to plug-and-play and learned regularization.

Common Mistakes

A first mistake is to think that data-driven methods do not use assumptions. They do. The assumptions are encoded in the data, architecture, loss, optimizer, and training procedure.

A second mistake is to think that a visually impressive reconstruction is automatically correct. Visual plausibility and measurement consistency are different.

A third mistake is to compare a learned method only to a weak baseline. A fair evaluation should include appropriate model-based methods, ablations, and tests under distribution shift.

A fourth mistake is to describe the network but not the training distribution. For learned imaging, the training set is part of the method.

Takeaways

  • Model-based methods write assumptions explicitly.
  • Data-driven methods learn assumptions from examples.
  • Neural networks are parameterized nonlinear operators.
  • Learned methods can be powerful but depend on training data.
  • Training data, loss, architecture, and augmentation encode assumptions.
  • Modern imaging often combines physics, optimization, and learning.
  • A learned reconstruction should be judged by both plausibility and consistency with measured data.
