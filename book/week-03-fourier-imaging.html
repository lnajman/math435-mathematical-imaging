Fourier Transform in Imaging

Chapter 3

Slides | Notebook | Open in Colab

Learning Goals

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • interpret a signal as a sum of oscillations;
  • read a 2D Fourier magnitude spectrum;
  • explain the tradeoff between frequency localization and spatial localization;
  • distinguish Fourier magnitude and phase;
  • state the convolution theorem;
  • explain blur and inverse filtering in the frequency domain.

The Big Idea

Fourier analysis rewrites a signal or image as a combination of oscillations.

Low frequencies describe slow variation. High frequencies describe rapid variation, edges, and texture.

For imaging, this matters because many forward operators act differently on different frequencies. Blur, for example, usually suppresses high frequencies more strongly than low frequencies.

This chapter gives the first deep explanation of instability. In Chapter 2, we saw that deblurring can amplify noise. Fourier analysis explains where that amplification comes from. Blur does not damage all components equally. It preserves some frequencies, weakens others, and may almost eliminate the finest oscillations.

If the forward model weakens a frequency component, then inversion must amplify that component to recover it. If noise is present in the same component, the noise is amplified too.

This is why Fourier analysis is not only a transform. In imaging, it is a diagnostic tool.

In the course arc, Fourier analysis is the first representation that lets us separate data-supported components from fragile components. It gives a language for saying which directions of the image space are transmitted strongly, which are transmitted weakly, and which are dangerous to reconstruct without regularization.

One-Dimensional Frequencies

A basic length-N oscillation is

s_k[n] = \sin(2\pi k n/N).

The integer k is the frequency index. A larger k means more oscillations over the same interval.

The Fourier magnitude reveals which oscillations are present.

The figure is a small dictionary. One sinusoid oscillates slowly; another oscillates quickly; their sum contains both. The Fourier magnitude identifies the active frequencies without caring where we happened to start drawing the signal.

This is useful because many imaging operations are easier to understand by asking which frequencies they keep, suppress, or amplify. A smoothing filter suppresses high frequencies. An edge detector emphasizes high frequencies. A deblurring filter tries to restore frequencies that blur has weakened.

The Discrete Fourier Transform

For a length-N signal x, the discrete Fourier transform is

\widehat{x}[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n]e^{-2\pi i kn/N}.

Each coefficient \widehat{x}[k] measures how much frequency k is present.

Fourier coefficients are complex:

\widehat{x}[k] = a_k + i b_k.

Their magnitude and phase are

|\widehat{x}[k]| \qquad\text{and}\qquad \arg(\widehat{x}[k]).

Magnitude measures strength. Phase measures alignment.

The DFT can be read as a change of coordinates. Instead of describing a signal by its values at positions, we describe it by its weights on oscillatory patterns. No information is lost when the full transform is kept. The representation changes, not the signal.

This coordinate-change view will reappear later with wavelets, DCT coefficients, sparse representations, and learned features. Each representation makes some structure easier to describe.

Orthogonality And Pythagoras

The Fourier transform is not just any change of coordinates. It is a projection onto orthogonal oscillatory directions.

In ordinary Euclidean geometry, if two vectors are orthogonal, the Pythagorean theorem says

\|a+b\|^2=\|a\|^2+\|b\|^2.

Fourier analysis extends this idea to many orthogonal directions. A signal can be decomposed into mutually orthogonal frequency vectors, and the squared norm of the signal is the sum of the squared magnitudes of its Fourier components, up to the normalization convention used for the transform.

This is the meaning of Parseval’s identity. With an orthonormal Fourier basis \{\phi_k\},

\|x\|_2^2 = \sum_k |\langle x,\phi_k\rangle|^2.

Each coefficient \langle x,\phi_k\rangle is the length of the projection of x onto one Fourier direction. Parseval says that the total energy is the sum of the energies of these orthogonal projections.

This is one of the deep links between Fourier analysis and the Pythagorean theorem: Fourier coefficients are not arbitrary measurements. They are coordinates along perpendicular axes in signal space.

Global Support

Fourier vectors are global. In a finite discrete image, each Fourier basis image extends across the whole image domain. In the continuous setting, sine and cosine waves have infinite support.

This has an important consequence. A local event, such as a sharp edge or a small bright object, cannot be represented by one local Fourier atom. It is spread across many global oscillations. Fourier gives excellent frequency localization, but poor spatial localization.

This is not a flaw; it is a tradeoff. Fourier analysis is ideal when the question is global frequency content, convolution, blur, periodic texture, or shift-invariant filtering. It is less natural when the question is where a local event occurs.

Wavelets in Chapter 11 respond to this limitation by using localized atoms. Many wavelets have compact spatial support, so they can represent events by position and scale as well as by frequency content.

Two-Dimensional Fourier Transform

For an image x[m,n], the 2D Fourier transform uses horizontal and vertical frequency indices:

\widehat{x}[u,v] = \sum_{m,n} x[m,n]e^{-2\pi i(um/M+vn/N)}.

The coefficient \widehat{x}[u,v] measures an oscillation with horizontal frequency u and vertical frequency v.

The centered log magnitude is a common way to display an image spectrum.

The logarithm in the display is important. Fourier magnitudes can vary over a large range, and without a log scale the strongest low-frequency values can hide weaker but meaningful high-frequency components.

When looking at a spectrum, do not expect it to look like the original image. It is not a picture of objects. It is a picture of oscillatory content: scale, direction, and strength.

Reading A Spectrum

In a centered spectrum:

  • the center corresponds to zero frequency and slow variation;
  • points far from the center correspond to fine oscillations;
  • direction in the spectrum corresponds to orientation in the image.

Strong vertical stripes in an image correspond to horizontal variation in intensity, so the spectrum is strong along the corresponding horizontal frequency direction.

The spectrum is especially useful for recognizing anisotropy. Motion blur in one direction, periodic texture, repeated stripes, and grid artifacts often leave directional signatures. This is one reason Fourier analysis remains useful even in the era of neural networks: it gives interpretable evidence about what is present in the data.

Note

A Fourier spectrum is not a neural feature map, but both are representations. The spectrum is hand-designed and interpretable; learned representations are data-adapted and often harder to interpret.

What The Spectrum Can And Cannot Say

A Fourier spectrum is evidence about frequencies, directions, and scales. It can tell us that an image contains strong oscillations at a certain orientation, or that a blur has suppressed high frequencies. It can reveal periodic artifacts, motion direction, grid effects, and the difference between smooth and textured regions.

But the spectrum is not a complete explanation of image content. Because Fourier atoms are global, the magnitude spectrum does not directly tell us where a local event occurs. Two images can have the same Fourier magnitude and different spatial organization. Phase helps recover geometry, but the frequency view still organizes information globally.

This distinction matters for inverse problems. A reconstruction may have the “right amount” of high-frequency energy and still put structures in the wrong place. Conversely, a reconstruction may look smoother than the original but preserve the structures that matter for a specific decision.

For students, a useful habit is to ask:

Question Fourier helps? Why
Which scales are present? yes frequency magnitude measures oscillatory strength
Is there directional texture or motion blur? yes orientation leaves spectral signatures
Where exactly is one edge? only indirectly global atoms do not localize events
Is a restored detail true? not by itself frequency evidence must be tied to the forward model

This is the bridge to later representations. Wavelets, sparse models, and neural feature maps all try, in different ways, to represent image structure with more spatial awareness.

Magnitude And Phase

Every Fourier coefficient can be written as

\widehat{x}[u,v] = |\widehat{x}[u,v]|e^{i\phi[u,v]}.

Magnitude says how much of a frequency is present. Phase says where that frequency is aligned.

Phase carries much of the recognizable geometry of an image.

Magnitude is visually important, but phase is often crucial for the location of edges and structures.

This is one of the most surprising facts for students. The magnitude tells us how much of each frequency exists, but phase tells us how those oscillations line up. Edges, contours, and recognizable geometry depend strongly on alignment.

For inverse problems, this matters because preserving energy at the right frequencies is not enough. A reconstruction also has to place structures correctly. A visually sharp but spatially wrong image may have impressive high-frequency content and still be unreliable.

The Convolution Theorem

The reason Fourier analysis is central to imaging is the convolution theorem:

\widehat{h*x}[u,v] = \widehat{h}[u,v]\widehat{x}[u,v].

Convolution in the image domain becomes multiplication in the Fourier domain.

If

y = h*x,

then

\widehat{y} = \widehat{h}\,\widehat{x}.

This theorem is the reason blur is often analyzed in frequency space. A large convolution matrix in the pixel domain becomes pointwise multiplication after the Fourier transform, at least under compatible boundary assumptions. Instead of one large coupled operation, each frequency can be studied separately.

This does not make the inverse problem disappear. It makes the difficulty visible.

There is also a modeling warning. The simplest Fourier-domain formula assumes boundary conventions that are compatible with circular convolution. Real images do not usually wrap around at the boundary. In practice, software choices such as padding, reflection, cropping, and windowing affect the spectrum and the deblurring result. These choices are part of the forward model, not cosmetic implementation details.

Blur In Frequency Space

A blur kernel usually has a Fourier transform that is large at low frequencies and small at high frequencies. That means

|\widehat{y}[u,v]| = |\widehat{h}[u,v]|\ |\widehat{x}[u,v]|

is much smaller where |\widehat{h}[u,v]| is small.

Blur is easier to analyze after the Fourier transform because frequencies are multiplied independently.

The quantity \widehat{h} is sometimes called the transfer function of the blur. Where |\widehat{h}| is near 1, the corresponding frequency passes through the imaging system. Where |\widehat{h}| is small, that frequency is strongly attenuated.

If a frequency is strongly attenuated, then the measured data contain only weak evidence about that part of the original image. Any method that restores it must rely partly on assumptions.

Low-Pass And High-Pass Filtering

A low-pass filter keeps slow variation and removes fine detail. A high-pass filter emphasizes rapid changes such as edges and texture.

Frequency filtering gives direct control over coarse and fine image components.

Low-pass filtering often makes an image look cleaner because it removes rapid fluctuations, including noise. But it also removes fine texture and can blur small structures. High-pass filtering can reveal edges, but it can also amplify noise and produce unnatural contrast.

This is a simple version of a tradeoff that will appear throughout the course: suppressing artifacts can also suppress information.

Why Inverse Filtering Is Dangerous

If \widehat{h}[u,v] is nonzero, a formal inverse filter would use

\widehat{x}[u,v] = \frac{\widehat{y}[u,v]}{\widehat{h}[u,v]}.

But when |\widehat{h}[u,v]| is small, division by \widehat{h} amplifies noise.

Inverse filtering recovers some detail but can amplify noise dramatically.

This frequency-domain view explains why regularization will be necessary in later chapters.

Regularized deblurring modifies the dangerous division. Instead of dividing fully by small values of \widehat{h}, it limits the amplification. Classical methods do this with explicit formulas or optimization penalties. Learned methods may do it implicitly by learning which recovered details are plausible.

The reliability question is the same in both cases. When a method restores high frequencies, are those frequencies supported by the measurement, or are they supplied mainly by the prior?

Common Mistakes

  1. Thinking high frequency means noise only. Edges and fine structures are also high-frequency content.
  2. Thinking low frequency means unimportant. Illumination, smooth shading, and large shapes are low-frequency content.
  3. Ignoring phase. Phase is essential for the placement of structures.
  4. Treating inverse filtering as a safe inverse. Division by small Fourier values is a mechanism for noise amplification.
  5. Forgetting boundary assumptions. Fourier deblurring formulas depend on how the image is extended beyond the recorded grid.

Takeaways

  • Fourier analysis represents images using oscillations.
  • Fourier coefficients are projections onto orthogonal global directions.
  • Parseval’s identity generalizes the Pythagorean theorem.
  • Fourier gives strong frequency localization but weak spatial localization.
  • Low frequencies describe coarse structure; high frequencies describe fine detail.
  • Fourier coefficients have magnitude and phase.
  • Convolution becomes multiplication in the Fourier domain.
  • Blur suppresses frequencies, and inverse filtering can amplify noise.
  • Fourier analysis prepares the later regularization question: which weakened components should be trusted, damped, or supplied by a prior?
