Motivation

From inverse problems to neural networks

The Central Question

This course is about images, but it is not mainly about displaying images. It is about recovering, interpreting, and trusting images from imperfect data.

The central question is:

Given measurements produced by an imaging system, what can we infer about the image or object that produced them?

This is an inverse problem. The data are not the object itself. The data are the result of a measurement process.

In its simplest form, we write

y = A x + \eta.

Here x is the unknown image or object, A is the forward model, y is the measured data, and \eta represents noise or error. The forward model tells us how an image would produce data. The inverse problem asks us to go in the opposite direction.

That opposite direction is usually difficult. Information may have been blurred, sampled, hidden, corrupted, or compressed. Some information may be weakened. Some may be missing entirely. A reconstruction method must therefore do more than invert a formula. It must use assumptions.

Why Images Are Different

Images are not just long signals. They are spatial objects. A pixel has neighbors. Edges have geometry. Textures have scale and orientation. Objects can occlude other objects. Small structures may matter.

This makes imaging richer than many one-dimensional signal problems. It also makes imaging more dangerous. A reconstruction can look visually plausible while being wrong in a way that matters.

For example, if pixels are missing from an already recorded image, we can model that as a mask and try to fill in the missing values. But if a person is physically hidden behind a tree, the camera did not measure the hidden part. A method may guess what could be behind the tree, but that guess is not recovered evidence. It is an assumption.

This distinction is one reason the course returns repeatedly to the same question:

Which part of the result comes from the data, and which part comes from the model?

Classical Reconstruction

Classical mathematical imaging often solves an optimization problem:

\hat{x} = \arg\min_x D(Ax,y)+\lambda R(x).

The term D(Ax,y) measures agreement with the data. It asks whether the reconstructed image, when passed through the forward model, produces measurements close to y.

The term R(x) expresses prior knowledge. It may prefer smooth images, sparse coefficients, small gradients, or piecewise-constant regions. The parameter \lambda controls the balance between fitting the data and trusting the prior.

This formula is not only a computational recipe. It is a statement of responsibility. Every reconstruction has assumptions, and those assumptions must be made visible.

The Move Toward Neural Networks

Neural networks enter naturally once the course has developed this language.

Instead of hand-designing a prior R(x), we may try to learn prior knowledge from examples. Instead of designing every step of an iterative algorithm, we may learn part of the reconstruction rule. Instead of writing a closed-form regularizer, we may use a trained denoiser inside an algorithm.

This gives several views of neural reconstruction:

View Mathematical form Interpretation
learned inverse map \hat{x}=f_\theta(y) learn the reconstruction directly from data
learned prior R_\theta(x) learn what plausible images look like
learned denoiser D_\theta(z) use a network as an image-cleaning operator
unrolled algorithm x^{k+1}=F_{\theta,k}(x^k,y) learn parts of an iterative solver
plug-and-play method x^{k+1}=D_\theta(x^k-\tau A^\top(Ax^k-y)) combine data consistency with a learned denoiser

This is why the course does not treat neural networks as a separate topic added at the end. Neural networks are one answer to questions that arise naturally from inverse problems:

  • What assumptions should we impose on unknown images?
  • Can those assumptions be learned from data?
  • How do we keep reconstructions consistent with measurements?
  • How do we detect hallucinations or failures?
  • How do we evaluate reliability under distribution shift?

What This Course Is Not

This course is not a general course on deep learning. It does not begin with architectures, optimizers, and benchmark datasets.

Instead, it asks what neural networks mean in an imaging context. A network that reconstructs an image is part of an inverse problem, explicitly or implicitly. It may replace an inverse operator, approximate a prior, act as a denoiser, or imitate an optimization algorithm.

That perspective changes the questions we ask. We do not only ask whether the output looks good. We ask:

  • What forward model is being inverted?
  • What data were measured?
  • What information was lost?
  • What prior knowledge was used?
  • Was the prior hand-designed or learned?
  • Is the output data-consistent?
  • What happens under noise, model mismatch, or domain shift?

How The Book Develops The Idea

The book follows the course arc.

Chapters 1-3 build the foundation: images as mathematical objects, blur as a forward model, and Fourier analysis as a way to see what blur removes.

Chapters 4-6 introduce noise, likelihoods, ill-posedness, and regularization. These chapters explain why reconstruction requires assumptions.

Chapters 7-9 show how assumptions become variational problems and numerical algorithms.

Chapters 10-13 introduce sparsity, wavelets, data-driven priors, and plug-and-play methods. This is where the bridge to neural reconstruction becomes explicit.

Chapter 14 asks how to trust any reconstruction, classical or learned.

The final project asks students to use these ideas in a concrete inverse problem, with LLM assistance required but ownership tested through experiments and oral defense.

The Habit Of Mind

The most important habit in the course is to read a reconstruction as an argument.

A reconstructed image argues that a certain unknown x is compatible with measured data y, a forward model A, a noise model, and a set of assumptions. The image may be beautiful, but beauty is not evidence by itself.

The course therefore trains two instincts at the same time:

  1. how to build reconstruction methods;
  2. how to question reconstruction methods.

That double instinct is essential for modern imaging. It is also essential for neural networks, because learned reconstructions can be powerful precisely where they can also be misleading.
