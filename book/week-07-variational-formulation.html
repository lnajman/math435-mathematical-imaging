Variational Formulation

Chapter 7

Slides | Notebook | Open in Colab

Learning Goals

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • interpret reconstruction as energy minimization;
  • separate data fidelity and regularization;
  • explain why convexity matters;
  • derive the Euler-Lagrange equation for a quadratic smoothing model;
  • connect optimality equations to gradient descent.

From One Model To A Framework

Tikhonov regularization solves

\min_x \|Ax-y\|_2^2+\lambda\|x\|_2^2.

This is one example of a larger modeling principle: choose an energy and minimize it.

In variational reconstruction, we write

\hat{x} = \operatorname*{argmin}_x E(x).

Often the energy has the form

E(x)=D(y,f(x))+\lambda R(x).

The data fidelity term D(y,f(x)) measures agreement with the observations. The regularizer R(x) encodes preferred image structure.

Energy As A Model

An energy is not only a numerical function. It is a modeling statement.

The minimizer balances data agreement with regularity.

This viewpoint separates three choices:

  • the data model decides the fidelity term;
  • the image prior decides the regularizer;
  • the optimization method decides how the energy is minimized.

Examples from previous chapters fit this pattern.

Model Energy
Gaussian direct denoising \|x-y\|_2^2
Tikhonov \|Ax-y\|_2^2+\lambda\|x\|_2^2
Quadratic smoothing \|u-y\|_2^2+\lambda\|\nabla u\|_2^2
Poisson data \sum_i f_i(x)-z_i\log f_i(x)

A First Variational Denoising Model

For a noisy image y, consider

E(u) = \frac12\|u-y\|_2^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2}\|\nabla u\|_2^2.

The first term keeps u close to the observed image. The second term penalizes changes between neighboring pixels.

The parameter \lambda controls the strength of smoothing:

  • small \lambda: the reconstruction stays close to the data;
  • large \lambda: the reconstruction becomes smoother;
  • too large \lambda: meaningful edges are blurred.

Convexity

Convexity is a geometric property of an energy. Informally, a function is convex if the line segment between two points on its graph stays above the graph.

Convex energies have no deceptive local minima.

For a convex energy:

  • every local minimizer is global;
  • first-order optimality conditions are meaningful;
  • many algorithms have reliable behavior.

Convex does not always mean easy, but it removes a major ambiguity. The quadratic denoising energy

E(u) = \frac12\|u-y\|_2^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2}\|\nabla u\|_2^2

is convex for \lambda\ge 0.

Euler-Lagrange Equation

We now derive the optimality condition for a continuous denoising energy:

E(u) = \frac12\int_\Omega (u-y)^2\,dx + \frac{\lambda}{2}\int_\Omega |\nabla u|^2\,dx.

The unknown u is the reconstruction, and y is the noisy image.

To test optimality, perturb u in a direction v:

u_\epsilon = u+\epsilon v.

At a minimizer, the derivative with respect to \epsilon must vanish at \epsilon=0:

\left. \frac{d}{d\epsilon}E(u+\epsilon v) \right|_{\epsilon=0} =0.

Variation Of The Data Term

For the data term,

\frac12\int_\Omega (u+\epsilon v-y)^2\,dx.

Differentiating at \epsilon=0 gives

\int_\Omega (u-y)v\,dx.

This says that the data term pushes u toward y.

Variation Of The Regularizer

For the smoothness term,

\frac{\lambda}{2}\int_\Omega |\nabla(u+\epsilon v)|^2\,dx,

the derivative at \epsilon=0 is

\lambda\int_\Omega \nabla u\cdot \nabla v\,dx.

Using integration by parts and ignoring boundary terms in this guided derivation,

\int_\Omega \nabla u\cdot \nabla v\,dx = -\int_\Omega (\Delta u)v\,dx.

The first variation becomes

\int_\Omega \left[(u-y)-\lambda\Delta u\right]v\,dx.

Since this must hold for all perturbations v,

(u-y)-\lambda\Delta u=0.

Equivalently,

u-\lambda\Delta u=y.

The minimizer solves a differential equation.

Discrete Version

On a pixel grid, the same idea leads to

(u-y)+\lambda L u=0,

where L is a discrete negative Laplacian.

For a finite-difference operator D, the energy

E(u) = \frac12\|u-y\|_2^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2}\|Du\|_2^2

has optimality condition

(u-y)+\lambda D^TDu=0.

Finite differences turn the continuous optimality equation into a grid equation.

This is Tikhonov with a different penalty: instead of penalizing \|x\|_2^2, we penalize squared image gradients.

Gradient Descent

The optimality equation gives a condition that the minimizer must satisfy. Gradient descent gives an iterative way to approach it.

For an energy E(u),

u^{k+1}=u^k-\tau\nabla E(u^k).

The parameter \tau is the step size.

Gradient descent moves locally downhill on the energy landscape.

For quadratic denoising,

\nabla E(u) = (u-y)+\lambda L u.

Therefore,

u^{k+1} = u^k-\tau\left[(u^k-y)+\lambda L u^k\right].

If \tau is too large, gradient descent can overshoot or diverge even for a convex quadratic.

The energy can decrease while image quality first improves and then becomes too smooth.

Real Image Denoising

Quadratic smoothing is transparent and useful. It is convex, its optimality condition is linear, and it reduces Gaussian-like noise.

Quadratic smoothing reduces noise, but it also blurs edges.

The weakness is also clear: edges are large gradients, and the term \|\nabla u\|_2^2 penalizes large gradients strongly. The next chapter replaces squared gradients with gradient magnitudes to better preserve edges.

Takeaways

  • Variational imaging models define reconstruction by energy minimization.
  • Energies combine data fidelity and regularization.
  • Convexity gives a safer optimization landscape.
  • Euler-Lagrange equations express first-order optimality.
  • Quadratic smoothness is clear and useful, but it blurs edges.
