Variational Formulation

Chapter 7

Slides | Notebook | Open in Colab

Learning Goals

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • interpret reconstruction as energy minimization;
  • separate data fidelity and regularization;
  • explain why convexity matters;
  • read an energy functional as a record of modeling choices;
  • derive the Euler-Lagrange equation for a quadratic smoothing model;
  • connect optimality equations to gradient descent.

From One Model To A Framework

Tikhonov regularization solves

\min_x \|Ax-y\|_2^2+\lambda\|x\|_2^2.

This is one example of a larger modeling principle: choose an energy and minimize it.

In variational reconstruction, we write

\hat{x} = \operatorname*{argmin}_x E(x).

Often the energy has the form

E(x)=D(y,f(x))+\lambda R(x).

The data fidelity term D(y,f(x)) measures agreement with the observations. The regularizer R(x) encodes preferred image structure.

Why This Chapter Matters

The previous chapters introduced several pieces separately: a forward model, a noise model, instability, and regularization. Variational formulation is the language that puts these pieces into one object.

Once a reconstruction is written as

\hat{x} = \operatorname*{argmin}_x D(y,f(x))+\lambda R(x),

we can ask precise questions:

  • what does it mean to fit the data?
  • what kinds of images does the regularizer prefer?
  • is the minimizer unique?
  • can we compute it reliably?
  • what will happen when the assumptions are wrong?

This is why variational methods remain useful even in a course that leads to neural networks. A neural network may replace the regularizer, approximate the optimizer, or learn the inverse map directly, but the same tension remains: the reconstruction must negotiate between data consistency and image plausibility.

Energy As A Model

An energy is not only a numerical function. It is a modeling statement.

The minimizer balances data agreement with regularity.

This viewpoint separates three choices:

  • the data model decides the fidelity term;
  • the image prior decides the regularizer;
  • the optimization method decides how the energy is minimized.

Examples from previous chapters fit this pattern.

Model Energy
Gaussian direct denoising \|x-y\|_2^2
Tikhonov \|Ax-y\|_2^2+\lambda\|x\|_2^2
Quadratic smoothing \|u-y\|_2^2+\lambda\|\nabla u\|_2^2
Poisson data \sum_i f_i(x)-z_i\log f_i(x)

The figure should be read as a compromise. The data term may prefer a candidate that stays close to a noisy measurement. The regularizer may prefer a smoother or simpler candidate. The total energy chooses the point where the two preferences balance.

This balance is not automatically correct. If the data term is wrong, the reconstruction may be faithful to the wrong measurement model. If the regularizer is wrong, the reconstruction may look clean but lose meaningful structures. If \lambda is poorly chosen, one term dominates the other.

The Three Modeling Choices

Variational reconstruction separates modeling from computation. This is one of its strengths.

Choice Typical question Example
Forward model how does x produce data? blur, masking, Fourier sampling
Data fidelity how should mismatch be measured? Gaussian least squares, Poisson likelihood
Regularizer what images are plausible? small norm, smoothness, TV, sparsity

The optimization algorithm is a fourth choice, but it should be chosen after the energy has been specified. Otherwise it becomes easy to confuse a modeling idea with the numerical method used to solve it.

Energy As Negotiation

It is helpful to read a variational model as a negotiation between terms.

Suppose two candidate reconstructions are possible:

  • x_1 fits the measurements very closely but contains many oscillations;
  • x_2 is smoother or simpler but does not fit every measurement exactly.

The energy

E(x)=D(Ax,y)+\lambda R(x)

does not know which candidate is “true” in an absolute sense. It only knows how to score candidates according to the two terms that we wrote down. The data term asks whether the candidate is compatible with the observed data and forward model. The regularizer asks whether the candidate looks plausible according to the chosen prior. The parameter \lambda decides how much disagreement between these two voices is tolerated.

This is why the same image data can lead to different reconstructions under different energies. A reconstruction method is not only a numerical recipe. It is a set of priorities.

Reading An Energy As An Audit Trail

An energy functional is an audit trail for the reconstruction.

For example,

E(x)=D(Ax,y)+\lambda R(x)

records at least four choices:

Part What it records
A what measurement process is assumed
D what noise or discrepancy model is assumed
R what image structures are preferred
\lambda how data fit and prior are balanced

This is why variational models are useful pedagogically. They force assumptions into visible mathematical locations. If a reconstruction fails, we can ask whether the forward model, data term, regularizer, parameter, or optimizer is responsible.

Learned methods often hide some of these choices inside training data, architecture, and loss functions. The variational viewpoint gives us a vocabulary for making those hidden choices visible again.

What The Energy Cannot See

The energy can only evaluate the information represented in its terms.

If the forward model is masking, pixels hidden behind the mask are not directly observed. The energy may still fill them in using a prior, but that fill-in is inferred structure, not recovered measurement. If an object is occluded by another object, the missing part is not secretly available to the algorithm. The method can guess using context, training data, or regularity assumptions, but the guess should not be confused with evidence.

This is a central difference between many image problems and idealized signal separation. In sound, under favorable assumptions, one may hope to separate mixed sources because the signals are superposed. In images, occlusion is often not a linear mixture. A hidden object may leave no measured trace at all.

The variational language makes this distinction visible. The data term says what was measured. The regularizer says how missing or unstable information is completed. Both are needed, but they have different epistemic status.

A First Variational Denoising Model

For a noisy image y, consider

E(u) = \frac12\|u-y\|_2^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2}\|\nabla u\|_2^2.

The first term keeps u close to the observed image. The second term penalizes changes between neighboring pixels.

The parameter \lambda controls the strength of smoothing:

  • small \lambda: the reconstruction stays close to the data;
  • large \lambda: the reconstruction becomes smoother;
  • too large \lambda: meaningful edges are blurred.

This model is deliberately simple. It is useful because every part can be understood. The data term is quadratic, the regularizer is quadratic, the energy is convex, and the optimality equation is linear.

It is also useful because its weakness is visible. Squared gradients penalize large changes strongly, so edges become expensive. The model does not know the difference between a small oscillation caused by noise and a strong transition caused by an object boundary. This limitation motivates Total Variation in the next chapter.

Convexity

Convexity is a geometric property of an energy. Informally, a function is convex if the line segment between two points on its graph stays above the graph.

Convex energies have no deceptive local minima.

For a convex energy:

  • every local minimizer is global;
  • first-order optimality conditions are meaningful;
  • many algorithms have reliable behavior.

Convex does not always mean easy, but it removes a major ambiguity. The quadratic denoising energy

E(u) = \frac12\|u-y\|_2^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2}\|\nabla u\|_2^2

is convex for \lambda\ge 0.

Convexity also clarifies what can go wrong in nonconvex learned models. Neural networks are usually trained with nonconvex objectives. Their success is practical and empirical, not guaranteed by the same convex theory. This does not make them invalid, but it means we need different forms of evidence: validation, robustness tests, ablation studies, and data consistency checks.

For this chapter, convexity gives us a clean laboratory. We can separate the conceptual role of the energy from the numerical complications of harder objectives.

Euler-Lagrange Equation

We now derive the optimality condition for a continuous denoising energy:

E(u) = \frac12\int_\Omega (u-y)^2\,dx + \frac{\lambda}{2}\int_\Omega |\nabla u|^2\,dx.

The unknown u is the reconstruction, and y is the noisy image.

To test optimality, perturb u in a direction v:

u_\epsilon = u+\epsilon v.

At a minimizer, the derivative with respect to \epsilon must vanish at \epsilon=0:

\left. \frac{d}{d\epsilon}E(u+\epsilon v) \right|_{\epsilon=0} =0.

Variation Of The Data Term

For the data term,

\frac12\int_\Omega (u+\epsilon v-y)^2\,dx.

Differentiating at \epsilon=0 gives

\int_\Omega (u-y)v\,dx.

This says that the data term pushes u toward y.

Variation Of The Regularizer

For the smoothness term,

\frac{\lambda}{2}\int_\Omega |\nabla(u+\epsilon v)|^2\,dx,

the derivative at \epsilon=0 is

\lambda\int_\Omega \nabla u\cdot \nabla v\,dx.

Using integration by parts and ignoring boundary terms in this guided derivation,

\int_\Omega \nabla u\cdot \nabla v\,dx = -\int_\Omega (\Delta u)v\,dx.

The first variation becomes

\int_\Omega \left[(u-y)-\lambda\Delta u\right]v\,dx.

Since this must hold for all perturbations v,

(u-y)-\lambda\Delta u=0.

Equivalently,

u-\lambda\Delta u=y.

The minimizer solves a differential equation.

This equation has a physical interpretation. The term u-y pulls the reconstruction toward the noisy image. The term -\lambda\Delta u diffuses or smooths the reconstruction. At equilibrium, data attraction and smoothing balance.

The derivation also shows why variational calculus is more than formal manipulation. Starting from a modeling principle, we obtain a condition that the reconstruction must satisfy. In simple cases, that condition can be solved directly. In harder cases, it guides the design of iterative algorithms.

Boundary Conditions

The integration-by-parts step hides a boundary choice. In imaging, the boundary behavior is not neutral. Common choices include:

  • periodic boundaries, where the image wraps around;
  • Neumann boundaries, where the normal derivative is zero;
  • Dirichlet boundaries, where values are fixed at the border;
  • reflective boundaries, often used to reduce visible edge artifacts.

Different choices can change the reconstruction near the image border. For small classroom examples this may look like a technicality. For real imaging tasks, boundary assumptions can create visible artifacts.

Discrete Version

On a pixel grid, the same idea leads to

(u-y)+\lambda L u=0,

where L is a discrete negative Laplacian.

For a finite-difference operator D, the energy

E(u) = \frac12\|u-y\|_2^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2}\|Du\|_2^2

has optimality condition

(u-y)+\lambda D^TDu=0.

Finite differences turn the continuous optimality equation into a grid equation.

This is Tikhonov with a different penalty: instead of penalizing \|x\|_2^2, we penalize squared image gradients.

The operator D^TD is a discrete version of a negative Laplacian. It spreads local information to neighboring pixels. This is why quadratic variational denoising looks like smoothing: each update or equation couples a pixel to its neighbors.

The discrete formula also explains why large dense matrices are usually avoided. We do not need to store D explicitly to apply finite differences. We can compute gradients, divergences, and Laplacians directly on the image grid.

Gradient Descent

The optimality equation gives a condition that the minimizer must satisfy. Gradient descent gives an iterative way to approach it.

For an energy E(u),

u^{k+1}=u^k-\tau\nabla E(u^k).

The parameter \tau is the step size.

Gradient descent moves locally downhill on the energy landscape.

For quadratic denoising,

\nabla E(u) = (u-y)+\lambda L u.

Therefore,

u^{k+1} = u^k-\tau\left[(u^k-y)+\lambda L u^k\right].

If \tau is too large, gradient descent can overshoot or diverge even for a convex quadratic.

The energy can decrease while image quality first improves and then becomes too smooth.

This last observation is subtle. The algorithm may be doing exactly what we asked: decreasing the chosen energy. But the image quality can still become worse if the energy does not perfectly match the desired task, or if the regularization weight is not appropriate. Optimization success and reconstruction success are related, but they are not identical.

This distinction will become important for learned models. A neural network can minimize its training loss very well and still fail on images that differ from the training distribution.

Optimality Is Not Truth

The phrase “optimal solution” can be misleading if it is detached from the model. A minimizer is optimal for a particular energy, not necessarily for the real-world task.

For a reconstruction \hat{x}, three statements are different:

Statement What it means
\hat{x} minimizes E the computation solved the chosen mathematical problem
\hat{x} fits the measurements the residual is small under the assumed forward model
\hat{x} is a faithful image the reconstruction preserves the structures that matter for the task

The first statement is about optimization. The second is about data consistency. The third is about interpretation. A good imaging workflow tries to check all three, especially when ground truth is unavailable.

This is why residual images, parameter sweeps, and sensitivity tests matter. They help us see whether the reconstruction is supported by the data or mainly imposed by the prior.

Real Image Denoising

Quadratic smoothing is transparent and useful. It is convex, its optimality condition is linear, and it reduces Gaussian-like noise.

Quadratic smoothing reduces noise, but it also blurs edges.

The weakness is also clear: edges are large gradients, and the term \|\nabla u\|_2^2 penalizes large gradients strongly. The next chapter replaces squared gradients with gradient magnitudes to better preserve edges.

When reading the figure, compare three regions separately: flat areas, edges, and texture. Quadratic smoothing often improves flat areas by reducing random fluctuations. It softens edges because edges have large gradients. It can damage texture because texture is made of many local changes, even when those changes are meaningful.

From Variational Models To Neural Models

Variational formulation gives a useful vocabulary for neural imaging.

Classical variational object Learned analogue
data term D(y,f(x)) data-consistency loss or physics layer
regularizer R(x) learned prior, denoiser, score, or generator
iterative optimizer unrolled network or learned update rule
parameter \lambda learned tradeoff or architecture-dependent balance

This does not mean every neural network secretly minimizes a known explicit energy. Some do, some do approximately, and some do not. The point is that variational thinking tells us what roles must be played: respecting the measurements, imposing image structure, and computing a reconstruction.

Common Mistakes

A first mistake is to think that an energy is objective truth. An energy is a model. It encodes choices, approximations, and priorities.

A second mistake is to confuse the minimizer of an energy with the true image. If the energy is poorly matched to the data or the task, the minimizer can be clean and wrong.

A third mistake is to ignore boundary conditions. Many artifacts near image borders come from assumptions made silently by convolution, finite differences, or Fourier transforms.

A fourth mistake is to discuss a reconstruction without naming the energy or loss that produced it. Without that, the assumptions are difficult to inspect.

Takeaways

  • Variational imaging models define reconstruction by energy minimization.
  • Energies combine data fidelity and regularization.
  • Convexity gives a safer optimization landscape.
  • Euler-Lagrange equations express first-order optimality.
  • An energy functional is also a record of assumptions.
  • Quadratic smoothness is clear and useful, but it blurs edges.
  • Variational thinking provides a vocabulary for learned priors and unrolled neural reconstruction.
