Bridge from Week 4

Week 4 gave us a data term:

D(y, f(x)).

If a candidate image fits the data well, does that mean it is the correct image?

The Inverse Problem

Forward problem:

x \longmapsto y = f(x).

Inverse problem:

y \longmapsto x.

The inverse problem asks us to recover a cause from an observed effect.

Linear Models Are a Special Case

When the imaging model is linear:

y = A x.

Examples include idealized blur, Fourier sampling, and recorded-pixel selection after image formation.

Physical scene occlusion is different: hidden content may never reach the sensor.

Part 1: Well-Posedness

Hadamard’s checklist

Three ways to fail

Hadamard Well-Posedness

The Three Questions

Existence

Does at least one solution fit the data?

Uniqueness

Is there only one such solution?

Stability

Do small data errors cause small solution errors?

Activity 1: Classify the Failure

5 minutes

For each case, which Hadamard condition may fail?

  1. The data are inconsistent with the model.
  2. A missing square can contain many different textures.
  3. A tiny amount of noise creates a wildly different reconstruction.

Well-Posed Does Not Mean Easy

A well-posed problem may still be computationally expensive.

An ill-posed problem may be small and fast but mathematically unreliable.

Well-posedness is about the behavior of the map from data to solution.

Part 2: Non-Uniqueness

Data do not always determine the image

Many causes, same observation

Missing Data Example

Same Observation, Different Images

Let M be a recorded-pixel selection mask.

y = Mx.

If two images differ only where M does not observe, then

Mx_1 = Mx_2.

The recorded data alone cannot choose between x_1 and x_2.

Important Distinction

Recorded-pixel masking:

a linear projection of an already formed image.

Physical occlusion:

hidden scene content may not be measured at all.

Both can create missing information, but they are not the same physical model.

Nullspace Language

For a linear operator A,

\mathcal{N}(A)=\{z:Az=0\}.

If z\in \mathcal{N}(A), then

A(x+z)=Ax.

The nullspace contains invisible changes.

Activity 2: Invisible Changes

5 minutes

Suppose an operator observes only every second pixel.

Describe one nonzero image vector z such that Az=0.

Consequence for Reconstruction

If A has a nontrivial nullspace, data fidelity cannot identify a unique image:

D(y, Ax) = D(y, A(x+z)).

What extra information could help choose one completion?

Part 3: Stability

Small errors, large consequences

The central inverse problem issue

Stability

An inverse map is stable if nearby data give nearby solutions.

Informally:

\|y_1-y_2\| \text{ small} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \|x_1-x_2\| \text{ small}.

Stability is the reason noise matters mathematically.

One-Dimensional Warning

Consider

y = \varepsilon x.

The inverse is

x = y/\varepsilon.

If \varepsilon is small, tiny errors in y become large errors in x.

Activity 3: Sensitivity

4 minutes

Let \varepsilon=0.001 and suppose the data error is 10^{-4}.

What is the error after inversion?

Imaging Version

Blur suppresses some directions in image space.

Deblurring tries to divide by those suppressions.

The unstable directions are the directions that the forward model almost erases.

Instability Picture

What Failed?

In the instability picture:

  • a solution exists;
  • the model is known;
  • the data error is tiny;
  • the direct inverse is still useless.

This is a stability failure.

Part 4: Singular Values

A coordinate system for sensitivity

Conceptual SVD

SVD Idea

For a linear operator A,

A v_i = \sigma_i u_i.

The singular value \sigma_i tells how strongly A transmits the input direction v_i.

Large and Small Singular Values

Large \sigma_i:

the direction is visible in the data.

Small \sigma_i:

the direction is weakly visible or nearly invisible.

Inverting in SVD Coordinates

If

y = A x

and

y = \sum_i \alpha_i u_i,

then the formal inverse is

x = \sum_i \frac{\alpha_i}{\sigma_i} v_i.

Where Noise Enters

With noisy data:

y^\delta = Ax + \eta.

The inverse includes

\frac{\langle \eta,u_i\rangle}{\sigma_i}.

Small singular values divide noise by a small number.

Singular Values of Blur

Reading the Plot

  • Stronger blur gives faster singular-value decay.
  • Later singular vectors often represent oscillatory detail.
  • Small singular values mean details are weakly observed.
  • Inversion becomes sensitive where singular values are small.

Coefficient View

Activity 4: SVD Diagnosis

6 minutes

You see singular values:

1,\; 0.7,\; 0.3,\; 0.02,\; 0.0001.

Which directions are dangerous to invert? Why?

Condition Number

For an invertible matrix,

\kappa(A)=\frac{\sigma_{\max}}{\sigma_{\min}}.

A large condition number means there is a direction that is transmitted much more weakly than another.

Condition Number Is Not the Whole Story

A large condition number warns us about worst-case sensitivity.

But in imaging we also care about:

  • where the noise lives;
  • whether small singular directions contain important image details;
  • how the reconstruction will be used.

Part 5: Stabilization Idea

Not solving yet

Just seeing the need

A First Stabilization Idea

If small \sigma_i amplify noise, one crude idea is:

\frac{\alpha_i}{\sigma_i} \quad\text{only for sufficiently large } \sigma_i.

This is the idea behind truncated SVD.

What Truncated SVD Does

Keeps stable directions that are well observed.

Discards unstable directions that may contain detail.

This is already a bias-stability tradeoff.

Preview of Regularization

Next week, instead of a hard cutoff, we will solve problems like

\min_x \|Ax-y\|_2^2 + \lambda \|x\|_2^2.

Regularization is a controlled way to trade exact data fit for stability.

What Students Should Remember

  • Ill-posedness is not just “hard”; it is a structural failure.
  • Missing information causes non-uniqueness.
  • Small singular values cause instability.
  • A direct inverse may fit the model and still be useless.
  • Regularization is motivated by the need for stable reconstruction.
