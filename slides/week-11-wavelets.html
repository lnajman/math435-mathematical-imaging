Bridge from Week 10

Week 10 said:

Sparse reconstruction depends on choosing a representation where the image has few important coefficients.

What representation should we choose for localized edges and details?

Fourier Is Global

A Fourier coefficient describes a global oscillation.

That is powerful for convolution and frequency filtering.

But a local edge affects many Fourier coefficients.

Global Versus Local Support

Fourier atoms

  • extend over the whole image;
  • give precise frequency information;
  • are global directions.

Wavelet atoms

  • can have compact or local support;
  • live at a position and a scale;
  • respond to local image structures.

Same Pythagoras, Different Geometry

In an orthonormal basis:

\|x\|_2^2 = \sum_j |c_j|^2.

Fourier coefficient:

projection on a global oscillation.

Wavelet coefficient:

projection on a localized pattern.

Both are orthogonal decompositions; they organize the same energy differently.

Localization Tradeoff

Representation Spatial support Frequency description
Fourier global very precise
Wavelets local or compact scale bands

If an image has one sharp edge, which representation should need fewer important coefficients?

Wavelets Are Local and Multiscale

Wavelets are patterns that can be:

  • shifted in position;
  • scaled in size;
  • used to describe coarse and fine image structures.

Part 1: Haar Intuition

The simplest wavelet

Average and difference

Haar Scaling and Wavelet Functions

Average and Detail

For two neighboring values a and b:

\text{average}=\frac{a+b}{2}, \qquad \text{detail}=\frac{a-b}{2}.

The average stores coarse information; the detail stores local change.

Repeat at Coarser Scales

Apply the same idea to averages:

  • first level: small local details;
  • second level: larger-scale details;
  • third level: even coarser structure.

Part 2: Multiscale Decomposition

Coarse plus details

One signal, several scales

1D Multiscale View

Reading the 1D Decomposition

  • The approximation contains the broad trend.
  • Coarse details capture large jumps.
  • Fine details capture narrow changes and oscillations.
  • Adding all components reconstructs the signal.

Energy Is Redistributed

The transform does not create new information.

It reorganizes information into:

\text{coarse approximation}+\text{details at several scales}.

For orthogonal wavelets, energy is preserved up to normalization.

Part 3: Images and Subbands

Horizontal, vertical, diagonal

2D wavelets

2D Wavelet Transform

For images, the transform is applied along rows and columns.

At each scale, we get:

  • approximation coefficients;
  • horizontal detail coefficients;
  • vertical detail coefficients;
  • diagonal detail coefficients.

Wavelet Coefficient Layout

Subband Interpretation

Subband Rough meaning
LL coarse approximation
LH horizontal-type detail
HL vertical-type detail
HH diagonal or corner-like detail

Naming conventions vary by library, but the idea is directional detail.

Part 4: Sparsity in Wavelet Domain

Few large coefficients

Localized detail is economical

Coefficient Decay

Why Wavelets Can Be Sparse

Many image regions are locally smooth.

Only edges and texture create large detail coefficients.

Wavelets often represent piecewise-smooth images with many small coefficients and a few large ones.

Compression by Keeping Large Coefficients

Hard Thresholding

Hard thresholding keeps a coefficient only if it is large enough:

H_T(c)= \begin{cases} c, & |c|\geq T,\\ 0, & |c|<T. \end{cases}

This is useful for compression, but it can create artifacts.

Soft Thresholding

Soft thresholding shrinks coefficients:

S_T(c)=\operatorname{sign}(c)\max(|c|-T,0).

Soft thresholding is the proximal operator behind l1 regularization.

Part 5: Wavelet Denoising

Small details may be noise

Threshold noisy coefficients

Denoising Idea

Noise often creates many small detail coefficients.

Wavelet denoising:

  • transforms the noisy image;
  • thresholds detail coefficients;
  • reconstructs the image.

Denoising Comparison

Threshold Strength

Choosing the Threshold

If the threshold is too small:

  • noise remains;
  • detail is preserved.

If the threshold is too large:

  • noise is removed;
  • real texture and small edges may disappear.

Wavelets Versus TV

Model Prior Typical strength Typical artifact
TV sparse gradients sharp edges staircasing
wavelets sparse multiscale coefficients localized detail ringing or texture loss

Both are sparse priors, but they encode different structure.

What Wavelets Do Not Solve

Wavelets are not automatic truth machines.

  • The wavelet family matters.
  • Boundary handling matters.
  • Texture may not be sparse.
  • Thresholds require tuning.
  • Inverse problems still need the forward model.

What Students Should Remember

  • Wavelets represent images across position, scale, and direction.
  • Wavelet transforms split images into coarse approximation and detail subbands.
  • Many images are approximately sparse in wavelet coefficients.
  • Thresholding gives compression and denoising algorithms.
  • Representation choice controls the success of sparse reconstruction.
