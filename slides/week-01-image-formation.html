What Is an Image?

At the mathematical level, an image is often modeled as a function

u : \Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}

where:

  • \Omega is the spatial domain
  • u(x,y) is the intensity at location (x,y)
  • color images use several channels, for example u : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}^3

From Continuous to Discrete

Digital images are sampled on a grid:

u_{i,j} \approx u(x_i, y_j)

For an image with m rows and n columns:

U = \begin{bmatrix} u_{1,1} & u_{1,2} & \cdots & u_{1,n} \\ u_{2,1} & u_{2,2} & \cdots & u_{2,n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ u_{m,1} & u_{m,2} & \cdots & u_{m,n} \end{bmatrix}

Matrix or Vector?

For visualization, a grayscale image is naturally a matrix:

U \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}

For linear algebra, we often reshape it as a vector:

x = \operatorname{vec}(U) \in \mathbb{R}^{mn}

This lets imaging problems use the tools of linear systems.

A First Forward Model

The central model of the course is:

y = A x + \eta

where:

  • x is the unknown clean image
  • A is the forward imaging operator
  • y is the observed data
  • \eta is noise or modeling error

Examples of Forward Operators

Operator Meaning
A = I direct observation
convolution matrix blur
subsampling matrix missing pixels or lower resolution
Fourier sampling MRI-style acquisition
projection operator tomography

Why This View Matters

Most imaging problems are inverse problems:

\text{given } y,\quad \text{recover } x

The naive answer would be:

x = A^{-1}y

But in real imaging, this often fails.

Why Inversion Fails

Three recurring difficulties:

  1. A may not be invertible.
  2. A may be badly conditioned.
  3. Noise \eta may be amplified by inversion.

This is the reason regularization is not a technical detail. It is the heart of reconstruction.

Python Representation

import numpy as np

m, n = 4, 5
U = np.arange(m * n).reshape(m, n)

x = U.reshape(-1)
U_back = x.reshape(m, n)

print(U)
print(x)

Row-Major Detail

NumPy stores arrays in row-major order by default:

U = np.array([[1, 2, 3],
              [4, 5, 6]])

print(U.reshape(-1))
# [1 2 3 4 5 6]

When implementing formulas, always state your vectorization convention.

A Toy Masking Operator

import numpy as np

x = np.linspace(0, 1, 12)
mask = np.array([1, 1, 0, 1, 0, 1, 1, 0, 1, 1, 0, 1], dtype=bool)

y = x[mask]

print("unknown image vector:", x)
print("observed entries:", y)

This is a very simple forward model: only some pixels are observed.

Modeling Habit

For every imaging problem, ask:

  1. What is the unknown?
  2. What is measured?
  3. What is the forward operator?
  4. What noise model is reasonable?
  5. What prior knowledge should guide reconstruction?

Takeaways

  • A digital image can be treated as both an array and a vector.
  • Image formation can often be written as y = Ax + \eta.
  • Reconstruction asks for x from incomplete, blurred, noisy, or indirect data.
  • The inverse problem viewpoint will organize the whole course.
